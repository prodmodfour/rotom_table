# UML Model

A model is a simplified representation of a system that emphasizes essential details and omits irrelevant ones. What counts as essential depends on the model's purpose and audience.

Models serve three critical functions:
- **Communication** — ensuring all parties share understanding of terminology, requirements, and decisions
- **Visualization** — presenting facts comprehensively to customers, experts, and users
- **Verification** — checking for completeness, consistency, and correctness through formal representation

A model without verification is worthless. Verification must be performed by knowledge carriers: employees, users, customers, business partners, domain experts, and management.

## Analysis process

Analysis consists of three stages:
1. **Obtaining** facts through collaboration between analysts and domain experts
2. **Representing** facts in diagrams and documents
3. **Verifying** facts by knowledge carriers

Useful techniques include observing employees at work, participating in processes, conducting interviews, brainstorming, and reviewing existing documentation.

## Models vs views vs diagrams

A single model contains multiple types of information. To manage complexity, it is split into [[uml-view|views]] — separate representations emphasizing different aspects. Each [[uml]] diagram is one view of the underlying model.
